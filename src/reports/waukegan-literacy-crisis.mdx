In 2024, just 10.6 percent of juniors at Waukegan CUSD 60 met the state benchmark for English Language Arts proficiency on the SAT. Statewide, the number was 31.1 percent. In neighboring Stevenson High School District, it was 71.8 percent. All three sit in Lake County, Illinois, separated by less than 20 miles of suburban road.

That gap is not an accident of a single bad year. It's the accumulated result of a district that has been demographically remade over nearly three decades while the systems around it haven't kept pace. Since 1997, Waukegan's student body has shifted from roughly half Hispanic to more than 80 percent. Its English Learner population has more than doubled in just seven years. And its SAT reading scores have been falling every year since the state started tracking them.

This report traces how Waukegan got here by following three threads: a statewide decline in reading that forms the backdrop, a local demographic transformation that reshaped the district's needs, and the widening performance gap that has placed Waukegan among the lowest-performing districts in the state.

## Illinois fourth-graders read no better today than they did in 2003

Before looking at Waukegan, it's worth understanding the state-level picture. Illinois participates in the National Assessment of Educational Progress, the only nationally standardized test administered across all 50 states. The NAEP reading assessment, given every two years to a sample of fourth and eighth graders, serves as a kind of background pulse check on whether kids are learning to read.

The pulse has been weakening for a decade.

Illinois fourth graders scored 214.2 on the NAEP reading assessment in 2024. That's lower than their score in 2003, when the state first broke 216. The brief upswing to 222 in 2015 has completely reversed. A similar but less severe pattern holds for eighth graders, where Illinois still outperforms the national average by about 5 points but has shed ground since 2017.

This is the statewide backdrop. Whatever is happening in Waukegan is happening on top of a reading crisis that's affecting the whole state. But in Waukegan, the statewide trend collides with a much more specific set of local conditions.

The most striking feature of Waukegan CUSD 60 over the past three decades isn't a test score. It's the speed and scale of the demographic change in its schools.

In 1997, Waukegan's schools were roughly 48 percent Hispanic, 29 percent Black, and 20 percent white. By 2025, Hispanic enrollment had climbed to 82 percent, white enrollment had dropped to under 3 percent, and Black enrollment was cut in half to 11 percent. The district didn't diversify; it underwent a near-complete ethnic replacement driven by immigration and white flight to surrounding suburbs like Lake Zurich, Libertyville, and Grayslake.

The most consequential dimension of this shift isn't race. It's language.

A 56 percent EL share doesn't just mean "more students need ESL classes." It means the majority of students in Waukegan are simultaneously learning English and learning to read in English. Those are different pedagogical challenges, but in an underfunded district they often collide in the same classroom. A student who arrives in third grade from Guatemala with strong Spanish literacy needs a very different instructional approach than a student born in Waukegan who has heard English at home but never had a parent read to them. Both end up classified as EL.

This surge happened while the district was also losing students. Enrollment has dropped 17 percent since 2018, from 16,275 to 13,551. The students who leave tend to be higher-performing families with resources to move. The students who arrive tend to need more support.

Waukegan's average SAT reading score has fallen every measured year, from 430 in 2019 to 406 in 2024, a 24-point drop. The state average also fell, from 498 to 482, but only 15 points. The gap widened from 67 points to 76. That's not convergence. That's divergence.

The 2024 proficiency data makes it even starker. Only 10.6 percent of Waukegan juniors met the ELA proficiency benchmark, compared to 31.1 percent statewide. For low-income students in Waukegan, the rate was 9.5 percent. Roughly 9 in 10 Waukegan juniors cannot read at the level the state considers proficient for their grade.

By 2025, the rate had improved to 35 percent but was still 10 points above the pre-pandemic baseline and 10 points above the state average. Students can't learn to read if they're not in school. And in a district where most students are already behind grade level, every missed day widens the gap.

The pandemic didn't create Waukegan's literacy problem. But it accelerated it. The 2019-to-2024 decline in SAT reading scores, from 430 to 406, maps almost exactly onto the period of peak absenteeism and disrupted instruction.

---

There's no single villain in this story. Illinois as a whole has been losing ground on reading for a decade. Waukegan's demographic transformation means the district serves a fundamentally different student body than it did 30 years ago, one with vastly greater language and literacy needs. The chronic absenteeism crisis stacked another layer of damage on top.

What the data makes clear is that 10.6 percent ELA proficiency is not a Waukegan problem that Waukegan can solve alone. It's the predictable outcome of a district absorbing enormous demographic change, a pandemic-era attendance collapse, and a statewide reading decline all at once, with the same resources and structures that served a very different community in the 1990s. In a county where the richest district's reading scores are 191 points above the poorest, the question isn't whether Waukegan is failing its students. It's whether the state is failing Waukegan.

## Data sources & methodology

**Datasets used:**
- `nces/naep` — State-level NAEP reading scores for Illinois and national public school averages, grades 4 and 8, 2003-2024
- `isbe/report-card` — School and district-level enrollment demographics, attendance rates, chronic absenteeism, and summative designations for Waukegan CUSD 60, 1997-2025. Pre-2018 data is school-level only; district aggregates computed as unweighted averages across schools
- `isbe/sat-scores` — District-level SAT reading scores, ELA proficiency rates, and score distributions for Waukegan CUSD 60, Lake County peers, and Illinois statewide, 2019-2024

**Calculations & transformations:**
Pre-2018 Waukegan demographics were computed as unweighted averages across all schools in the district for each year, since district-level rollup rows only exist from 2018 onward. This may slightly over- or under-represent smaller schools. SAT reading gap calculations are simple differences between Waukegan and statewide averages. No inflation adjustments or statistical modeling were applied.

**Limitations:**
- No ISAT (1999-2014) or PARCC/IAR (2015-present) data is available in the API, so grades 3-8 reading proficiency at the district level cannot be trended. The SAT data covers only high school juniors.
- ELA proficiency rates (`sat_ela_proficiency_rate_total`) are only populated for 2024; prior years have null values, limiting proficiency trend analysis to a single year.
- 2020 is missing from both the SAT and chronic absenteeism data due to COVID testing cancellations and changes in how remote attendance was counted.
- Chronic absenteeism in 2020 (16.4 percent for Waukegan) is artificially low because remote "attendance" was counted differently and is excluded from trend analysis.
- Pre-2018 demographic percentages are school-level averages, not enrollment-weighted, which may not perfectly match the true district-wide composition.

*Data accessed on 2026-04-01 via the [OpenData API](https://tryopendata.ai).*
