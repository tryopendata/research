In 2023, Texas property-wealthy school districts sent $4.5 billion back to the state through recapture, the wealth-equalization mechanism critics call "Robin Hood." That same year, charter school enrollment crossed 400,000 students for the first time, each one funded almost entirely by state dollars.

The two trends are not a coincidence. They are two halves of the same financial loop.

Texas school finance runs on a simple idea: local property taxes fund schools, and the state takes excess revenue from wealthy districts to redistribute to poorer ones. But as property values climb and charter schools pull students out of traditional districts, that system is producing results nobody designed. Districts lose students to charters but keep the same property tax base, which pushes their wealth-per-student higher, which triggers more recapture, which sends more money to the state, which funds more charter schools. Rinse, repeat.

The numbers tell the story of a system eating its own tail.

## Recapture has tripled in 15 years

When Texas created the recapture system in 1993, 34 districts sent a combined $131 million to the state. By 2023, 299 districts were writing checks totaling $4.5 billion, a 34-fold increase. Even adjusting for inflation and enrollment growth, the trajectory is staggering.

The 2019 reform bill (HB 3) was supposed to fix this. It injected $6.4 billion into school finance and temporarily cut recapture by 29%. But the relief was short-lived. Rising property values, especially the Permian Basin oil boom, pushed recapture past its pre-reform peak within three years.

The 2024 dip to $2.7 billion reflects property tax rate compression and legislative adjustments, not a structural fix. TEA projects recapture will climb back above $3 billion in 2025.

In 2024, Austin ISD sent $661 million in recapture to the state. That's 96% of every dollar the district collected in local property taxes. Put another way: for every dollar Austin homeowners paid in school property taxes, 96 cents left the district.

The list of top payers splits into two categories. Suburban ISDs with expensive real estate (Austin, Plano, Eanes, Highland Park, Lake Travis) and small West Texas oil patch districts (Pecos-Barstow-Toyah, Midland) where a handful of drilling rigs can push property wealth per student into the tens of millions.

That growth came almost entirely from traditional ISDs. While charter enrollment added 364,000 students, traditional ISD enrollment grew by just 481,000 over the same period, and actually dipped below its 2019 level after the pandemic and never fully recovered. Some of that ISD plateau is demographic (Texas birth rates have declined), but a significant share is students crossing over to charters.

## Charters get more state money per student than traditional districts

The funding structure creates an imbalance. Because charter schools have zero local revenue, the state must cover their entire per-pupil cost. In 2025, the state spent $10,973 per charter student compared to $5,181 per traditional ISD student. Total per-pupil revenue is closer (charters get $13,462 vs. ISDs' $12,598), but the state's share of the tab is wildly different.

This isn't because charter students are more expensive to educate. It's because the state is the sole funder. Traditional ISDs split the bill between local property taxes and state aid. Charters can't. The result: every student who moves from an ISD to a charter costs the state roughly twice as much in state dollars, even though the total per-pupil cost is similar.

In 2025, with 435,000 charter students generating roughly $10,973 each in state aid, the state's charter tab comes to about $4.8 billion. That's a significant share of the $31.2 billion the state spent on all public education.

## The feedback loop: fewer students, more recapture

Here's where the two trends collide. When a student leaves a traditional ISD for a charter school, the district loses the state funding that followed that student. But the district's property tax base doesn't change. A house in Austin is worth what it's worth whether the kid next door attends the neighborhood school or a charter across town.

So the district's property wealth per student goes up, which triggers more recapture. The district now sends more money to the state while serving fewer students.

In real terms, that $6,160 is worth closer to $4,900 today. Districts are being asked to educate students with less purchasing power per head while simultaneously writing larger recapture checks. The 89th Legislature (2025) ended its session without increasing the allotment.

The freeze matters because it affects how much revenue a district is "entitled" to keep before recapture kicks in. A higher allotment would raise the threshold and reduce recapture for many districts. Keeping it frozen while property values rise is a policy choice that mechanically increases recapture collections.

The pattern is consistent. The wealthiest districts are tiny oil and gas communities in the Permian Basin. The urban districts like Austin, Plano, and Dallas that dominate the absolute dollar totals are property-wealthy for a different reason: expensive real estate in booming metros.

## Some districts send back more than they collect

The most extreme cases are in West Texas. Grady ISD, with 249 students, sends back nearly five times its local tax collections in recapture. Rankin ISD (294 students) sends back 4.6 times. These tiny districts generate enormous property tax revenue from oil and gas operations but have so few students that their per-pupil wealth triggers massive recapture obligations.

This is possible because recapture is calculated on total taxable property value relative to the number of students, not on the district's actual tax collections. A district like Grady ISD collects $11.9 million in local property taxes but owes $58.9 million in recapture. The state essentially treats the district's oil wealth as belonging to the system, not the district.

Austin ISD is a different animal. At 96%, it's not technically over 100%, but it's close enough that the practical effect is the same: nearly every local tax dollar leaves the district. For a city of Austin's size, with 73,000 students and all the fixed costs of running a major urban school system, that leaves very little room to maneuver.

---

The political tension around recapture has been building for years. In 2023, several North Texas ISDs, including Keller and Carroll, voted to stop making recapture payments in protest, though they ultimately complied.

The May 2025 passage of Senate Bill 2, which created $10,300 education savings accounts (vouchers) for private schools, adds another variable. Vouchers follow the same financial logic as charter schools: students leave traditional districts, enrollment drops, property wealth per student rises, recapture grows.

If Texas follows the trajectory of the last decade, the system will keep concentrating more financial pressure on the districts that can least afford it. The basic allotment hasn't been raised since 2019. Property values continue to climb. Charter enrollment keeps growing.

Austin ISD has paid $7.4 billion since 2001. By 2030, it will have paid more than $10 billion.

The question isn't whether recapture should exist. Equalizing funding across wealthy and poor districts is a legitimate and necessary goal. The question is whether a system designed in 1993 can handle the pressures of 2026: rising property values, frozen per-student funding, and a rapidly growing charter sector that receives state funding without contributing to local tax bases.

Right now, the math says it can't.

## Data sources & methodology

**Datasets used:**
- `tea/recapture` - Statewide recapture payments by district, 1994-2025 (2025 are TEA projections, not actuals)
- `tea/summarized-finance` - PEIMS financial data by district, 2009-2025. Revenue and enrollment by district type.
- `tea/basic-allotment` - Historical per-student basic allotment amounts, 1984-2026
- `tea/wealth-per-wada` - Property values per weighted average daily attendance, current snapshot
- `tea/district-directory` - District classification (charter vs. independent ISD) used for enrollment and finance breakdowns

**Calculations & transformations:**
- Charter vs. traditional ISD classification was determined by joining `tea/summarized-finance` with `tea/district-directory` on district ID, using the `district_type` field
- Per-pupil state revenue calculated as total state revenue divided by fall survey enrollment for each district type
- Recapture as percentage of local tax revenue calculated as recapture payment divided by all-funds local M&O tax revenue per district
- Statewide charter state funding estimate (approximately $4.8 billion) calculated as charter enrollment (434,986) multiplied by per-pupil state revenue ($10,973)

**Limitations:**
- The district directory is a current-year snapshot, not a historical time series. Districts that converted to or from charter status during 2009-2025 may be misclassified for earlier years. The impact is likely small given the stability of district types.
- 2025 recapture figures are TEA projections, not final actuals
- The recapture-as-percentage-of-local-tax calculation for oil patch districts exceeds 100% because recapture is calculated on total taxable property value, which can exceed actual tax collections when the recapture formula determines a higher obligation than local M&O rates generate
- Federal revenue spikes in 2022-2023 reflect one-time ESSER/COVID relief funds, not structural funding increases
- Inflation adjustment claims (25% cumulative since 2019) are based on CPI-U general index, not education-specific cost indices
