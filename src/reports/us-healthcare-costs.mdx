Every year, the United States spends $13,473 per person on healthcare. Switzerland, the next most expensive country, spends $10,602. Japan spends $5,365. The median wealthy nation spends about $7,300. By any measure, America is the most expensive healthcare system on Earth, and the gap is widening.

What does that money buy? Less than you'd think. Among ten comparable democracies, the US ranks dead last in overall healthcare system performance, dead last in health outcomes, and dead last in access to care. Americans live shorter lives, lose more mothers in childbirth, and bury more children than citizens of any peer nation. The problem isn't the quality of American medicine when you can get it. The problem is a system where administrative overhead alone costs more per person than most countries spend on all of healthcare combined.

## America outspends every peer nation, and the gap keeps growing

In 2000, the US spent $4,549 per person on healthcare, roughly $1,200 more than Switzerland. By 2023, that gap had ballooned to $2,871. While every wealthy nation saw healthcare costs rise, the American trajectory pulled away from the pack like a breakaway cyclist who never looked back.

## More money, worse results

The cruelest part of America's healthcare story isn't the cost. It's what the cost buys. Among wealthy democracies, the US is the outlier that breaks the expected pattern: more spending should mean better outcomes. Instead, the US occupies a lonely quadrant on the chart below, far to the right on spending and well below the trend line on life expectancy.

Japan spends $5,359 per person and delivers 84.4 years of life expectancy. The US spends $6,721 more per person and gets 8.1 fewer years. That's not a rounding error. It's a systemic failure.

The gap is getting worse. In 1990, Americans lived about 1.7 years less than the average citizen of a comparable wealthy nation. By 2022, that gap had nearly tripled to 5 years. While peers gained an average of 5.5 years of life expectancy over three decades, Americans gained just 2.2.

The outcomes picture is equally stark at the start and end of life. The US has the highest child mortality rate of any wealthy nation in this comparison, at 0.65 deaths per 1,000 live births. American maternal mortality tells an even more striking story: 21.1 deaths per 100,000 live births, roughly double Canada's rate, five times Germany's, and twelve times Norway's.

That $1,076 figure is 4.7 times Japan's and nearly 12 times the UK's. It translates to roughly $280 billion in "excess" administrative spending annually compared to the peer average. To put that in context: $280 billion is enough to fully fund the National Institutes of Health seven times over.

The reason is structural. The US has over 900 private insurers, each with its own formularies, prior authorization rules, billing codes, and claims processes. Hospitals employ armies of coders, billers, and compliance staff to navigate this maze. A 2019 study in the *Annals of Internal Medicine* found that administrative costs accounted for 34% of total US healthcare expenditure. In Canada's single-payer system, that figure was 17%.

In 2024, the "Net Cost of Health Insurance" line in the National Health Expenditure accounts hit $306 billion. This measures insurer overhead, profit, and reserves. Not a single dollar of that represents medical care. It's the toll charged for the privilege of having a middleman between you and your doctor.

The scale of individual drugs is eye-watering: in 2023 alone, Medicare Part D spent $18.3 billion on Eliquis (a blood thinner), $9.2 billion on Ozempic, and $8.8 billion on Jardiance. This relentless inflation means healthcare consumes an ever-larger share of household budgets, government spending, and business costs. In 1960, the US spent just $146 per person on healthcare. By 2024, that number hit $15,474, a 106-fold increase in nominal terms. Even adjusted for inflation, real per-capita spending roughly tripled since 1980.

The Affordable Care Act brought 17 million newly insured Americans into the system between 2010 and 2015, dropping the uninsured rate from 15.5% to 9.4%. But expanding coverage into a system with unchecked price growth is like running a bathtub with the drain open. Today, 27.5 million Americans still lack insurance, and those who have it face deductibles, copays, and premiums that strain family budgets.

The two orange bars, Insurance Admin ($306B) and Government Administration ($66B), represent $372 billion in combined overhead. That's 7.4% of total healthcare spending that goes to processing claims, managing enrollment, marketing insurance plans, and regulatory compliance rather than treating patients. It's roughly the entire GDP of Hong Kong.

Here's the paradox buried in the funding data: even though the US government covers "only" 54% of healthcare spending (the lowest among peers except Switzerland), its per-capita government healthcare expenditure already exceeds what most countries spend in total. Medicare, Medicaid, the VA, and other programs are so expensive per beneficiary that even the public half of America's system costs more than universal healthcare does in peer nations. The problem isn't that the government doesn't spend enough. It's that the system it's paying into charges too much.

---

The fundamental question isn't whether the US can afford to fix its healthcare system. It's whether it can afford not to. At $5.3 trillion and climbing, American healthcare absorbs resources that could fund infrastructure, education, or simply stay in workers' paychecks. The system produces world-class clinical care for those who can navigate it, but wraps that care in layers of administrative complexity, unregulated pricing, and access barriers that leave Americans sicker and poorer than their peers.

## Data sources & methodology

**Datasets used:**
- `owid/healthcare-spending` — Per-capita health spending (inflation-adjusted USD), 206 countries, 2000-2023
- `who/health-expenditure-by-source` — Health spending as % of GDP, government/private/OOP shares, 2000-2023
- `oecd/health-expenditure-by-function` — Spending by function category (curative, admin, pharma), USD PPP, 43 countries, 2023
- `oecd/pharma-spending` — Pharmaceutical spending per capita (USD PPP), 42 countries, 2023
- `worldbank/life-expectancy` — Life expectancy at birth, 1960-2022
- `owid/child-mortality` — Under-5 mortality per 1,000 live births, 2021
- `owid/maternal-mortality` — Maternal mortality per 100,000 live births, 2020
- `commonwealth/mirror-mirror` — Healthcare system rankings across 10 wealthy democracies, 2024
- `cms/health-expenditure` — US National Health Expenditure per capita, 1960-2024
- `cms/nhe-by-category` — US NHE by service category, 1960-2024
- `fred/cpi-medical` and `fred/cpi` — Consumer Price Index for medical care and all items, 1947-present
- `census/health-insurance-coverage` — US uninsured rates by state, 2008-2024
- `cms/part-d-spending` — Medicare Part D spending by drug, 2023

**Calculations & transformations:**
- CPI comparison indexed both medical and all-items CPI to 100 at 1980 (January values for each year)
- "Excess" per-capita spending estimated by comparing US per-capita figures to the unweighted average of Germany, France, UK, Japan, Australia, Canada, and Switzerland across OECD function categories
- The synthesis chart's "Hospital & clinical prices" excess is a residual: total US excess minus admin excess minus pharma excess
- GDP share gap ($1.4 trillion) calculated as the difference between US 16.7% and Germany/Switzerland 11.7%, applied to 2023 US GDP (~$28T)

**Limitations:**
- Cross-country spending comparisons use different adjustment methods (inflation-adjusted USD vs USD PPP) depending on the source. OWID uses inflation-adjusted nominal; OECD function data uses PPP. Both are standard but not directly comparable
- Maternal mortality uses modeled WHO estimates with uncertainty bands, especially for earlier years
- NHE category data is in nominal dollars, not inflation-adjusted
- No clean cross-country physician salary data was available through the API; this known cost driver is addressed qualitatively
- The 2020-2021 data across all datasets reflects COVID-era distortions (emergency spending, GDP contraction, excess mortality)
