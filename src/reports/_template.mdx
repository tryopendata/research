{/*
  Report Template
  ===============
  Copy this file and rename it to create a new report.
  The filename becomes the URL slug (e.g., us-economic-health.mdx -> /us-economic-health).

  Required: export const meta with title, date, description, tags.
  The comments below explain the narrative structure, chart design principles, and anti-patterns.


  ============================================================
  I. NARRATIVE ARCHITECTURE
  ============================================================

  A data report is an argument, not a dashboard. Every section advances
  a claim, and every chart is evidence for that claim.

  STRUCTURE (the "Data Storytelling Arc"):
  1. HOOK — Open with the most surprising or consequential finding.
     Not methodology ("We analyzed 2.3M records"). Not a truism
     ("In today's economy..."). A specific, concrete finding that
     makes the reader want to know why.
     Good: "One in four Illinois students is chronically absent."
     Good: "Solar grew 62x in 14 years, but emissions still rose."
     Bad:  "This report examines attendance trends in Illinois."

  2. NUT GRAF — One paragraph establishing what this report covers,
     why it matters now, and what's at stake. This is the reader's
     contract: they now know what they'll get by reading further.

  3. SECTIONS (3-5, each follows the Claim-Chart-Context-Consequence pattern):
     - Claim:       One sentence stating the section's finding (this becomes the ## heading)
     - Chart:       Visual evidence supporting the claim
     - Context:     Interpretation — what the data means, not just what it shows
     - Consequence: Why it matters, or what question it raises for the next section

     Each section should flow into the next. The consequence of section 2
     raises the question that section 3 answers. This creates narrative
     momentum rather than a disconnected list of findings.

  4. CLOSING — Tie threads together. Three strong patterns:
     - Forward look: project the trend ("At current rates, X reaches Y by 2030")
     - Callback: return to the opening finding with new context
     - Quiet statement of fact: one final number, stated plainly, no interpretation needed

     Don't end with caveats or methodology. End with meaning.
     (Methodology details go in the dedicated "Data sources & methodology"
     section that follows the closing — see the bottom of this template.)

  PACING:
  - Never stack two charts back-to-back without narrative prose between them.
    The reader needs text to process each chart before encountering the next.
  - After your most important chart, give it a full paragraph of interpretation.
    Let the finding breathe.
  - Front-load your most compelling visual. Readers have the most attention early.
  - Alternate "revelation" beats (here's what the data shows) with "reflection"
    beats (here's what it means). Revelation is fast; reflection is slower.
  - 300-500 words per section is a good target. Under 200 feels underdeveloped.
    Over 700 means the section is probably making two points and should be split.

  PROSE STYLE:
  - Lead with findings, not methodology.
  - Use specific numbers: "$31.1 trillion" not "increased significantly."
  - Connect numbers to human scale: "A percentage point doesn't sound like much,
    but in a labor force of 168 million, it represents 1.7 million people."
  - No jargon without context. If you use CPI, explain it means the cost
    of a standard basket of goods.
  - Every standalone number needs a comparison: historical anchor ("not seen
    since 2008"), per-person math ("$1,200 per household"), peer comparison
    ("ranks 28th of 36 OECD nations"), or human scale.
  - State findings confidently. Caveat specific measurements inline with
    numerical ranges when needed. Put methodology details at the end, not
    woven into every sentence. Over-caveating ("could potentially suggest
    a possible trend") drains authority.

  THE PROSE-CHART CONTRACT:
  - Prose sets up the chart (what question does it answer?)
  - The chart provides visual evidence (it should be interpretable standalone)
  - Prose interprets the chart (what does it mean, not what does it show)
  - NEVER write "As shown in Figure 3..." or "The chart below illustrates..."
    State the finding in prose. Let the chart prove it visually. They reinforce
    each other but don't depend on each other.
  - NEVER describe what the chart shows ("the line goes up"). The reader has eyes.
    Instead, add context the chart can't: "That 3% drop represents 4.2 million people."


  ============================================================
  II. CHART DESIGN PRINCIPLES
  ============================================================

  CHOOSE THE RIGHT CHART (start with the relationship, not the chart type):
  - Deviation from a reference:  diverging bar, surplus/deficit line
  - Correlation:                 scatter, connected scatter, bubble
  - Ranking:                     bar (horizontal), slope, lollipop
  - Distribution:                histogram, dot strip, box plot
  - Change over time:            line (1-5 series), area (volume), column (discrete periods)
  - Part-to-whole:               donut (2-6 categories), stacked bar, treemap
  - Magnitude comparison:        bar, column, lollipop
  - Spatial:                     choropleth, proportional symbol map

  COLOR IS NARRATIVE, NOT DECORATION:
  - Highlight + gray is the default. Color the protagonist, gray the context.
    When everything is colored, nothing stands out.
  - Use semantic color: red for decline/danger/heat, green for growth, blue
    for neutral. A temperature chart should feel warm. A debt chart should
    feel alarming. If your colors are arbitrary, you're leaving narrative
    power on the table.
  - Diverging palettes when data crosses a meaningful threshold (zero, a
    target, a baseline). The midpoint must be semantically meaningful, not
    just the mathematical center.
  - 1 color + gray covers most editorial work. 2-3 for direct comparisons.
    4-5 is the max for categorical. 6+ means regroup into "Other" or use
    small multiples.
  - Never ship all-blue or rainbow charts without a reason. Ask: what does
    the color communicate about the data?

  TITLES ARE ASSERTIONS, NOT LABELS:
  - Chart titles state the finding: "GDP surpassed $31T in mid-2025"
  - NOT a description of contents: "GDP Over Time"
  - Subtitles carry metadata: units, time range, geography
  - Keep the title under 10 words. It should prime the reader for what to
    look for in the chart.
  - The title and the visual should agree. If the title says one thing but
    the chart's visual hierarchy emphasizes something else, fix one of them.

  ANNOTATIONS EARN THEIR PLACE:
  - Annotate when the reader would ask "what happened here?" at a spike,
    inflection, crossover, or outlier.
  - Use range annotations (shaded regions, opacity 0.1-0.15) for notable
    periods: recessions, policy changes, pandemics.
  - Use reference lines for meaningful thresholds: zero, targets, averages,
    historical baselines. Dashed/dotted to distinguish from data lines.
  - Label key data points directly on the chart, especially endpoints on
    line charts and outliers on scatter plots.
  - Budget: 0-3 per chart. More means the chart is telling too many stories.
    Split it.
  - Annotation copy: short noun phrases, not sentences. "Pandemic lockdowns"
    not "This is when pandemic lockdowns began."

  THE 5-SECOND TEST:
  - Cover the title. Look at the chart. Can you state the takeaway in
    5 seconds? If not, the visual hierarchy needs work.
  - Uncover the title. Does it match what you saw? If not, either the
    title or the chart needs to change.
  - The "and" test: if you need "and" to describe what a chart shows
    ("GDP growth AND unemployment AND inflation"), it's doing too much.

  GIVE CONTEXT — "COMPARED TO WHAT?":
  - A number without a reference point is meaningless. Is 4.2% unemployment
    good or bad? Is $31T GDP high or low?
  - Add historical baselines ("pre-pandemic level" dashed line)
  - Add averages/medians ("national average" reference line)
  - Add targets/thresholds ("2% inflation target")
  - Add peer comparisons (gray "OECD average" line behind the country profile)
  - The chart and the prose should both provide context, through
    complementary channels.

  CHART-SPECIFIC GUIDANCE:
  - Line: use labels: { density: "endpoints" } so readers see start/end values.
    Highlight one series, gray the rest when one trend is the story.
  - Bar: sort by value for rankings. Use diverging color when values cross zero.
    Bars MUST start at zero (bar length encodes magnitude).
  - Scatter: label notable outliers directly with text annotations. Use size
    for a third variable. Include a reference line at a meaningful threshold.
  - Area: use for volume emphasis and composition shifts. Give the most
    important layer the strongest visual weight.
  - Donut: 2-6 categories max. Highlight the one slice that's the story.


  ============================================================
  III. ANTI-PATTERNS
  ============================================================

  NARRATIVE:
  - Opening with methodology instead of a finding
  - Describing what a chart shows instead of what it means
  - Sections that don't connect — each should raise a question the next answers
  - Including a chart because you made it during analysis, not because it
    advances the argument (exploratory work masquerading as explanatory)
  - Hedging everything: "could potentially suggest a possible trend"
  - Charts that exist because "the article needs a visual" rather than
    because they support a point in the story

  VISUAL:
  - Truncated y-axis on bar charts (exaggerates differences)
  - Dual y-axes (almost always misleading — the creator controls the visual
    relationship by choosing where each axis starts and ends)
  - Rainbow color palette when 2-3 colors would suffice
  - Pie charts with more than 6 slices
  - Legend-dependent reading (label directly on the chart instead)
  - Inconsistent scales across related charts that invite visual comparison
  - More than 6 charts in one report (you're not editing enough)
  - Over 100 rows of inline data per chart (over 500 is a bloat problem)

  DATA:
  - A number without comparison or context
  - Jargon without translation
  - Aggregates that hide important variation ("the national average masks...")
*/}

  4. KNOWN LIMITATIONS — Data gaps, suppressed values, changes in methodology
