In September 2025, one in every 36 federal workers quit. That monthly quits rate of 2.8 percent was 4.7 times the long-run average and higher than anything the Bureau of Labor Statistics had recorded in the previous 13 years of tracking. It wasn't a normal month. It was the culmination of a year in which the federal government simultaneously froze hiring to its lowest sustained rate on record and watched job openings fall by half.

The aviation system sits directly in the blast radius. Air traffic controllers, TSA screeners, FAA safety inspectors — these are federal employees. They can't be replaced quickly. A new air traffic controller takes two to four years to certify. A TSA officer needs months of training. When the government shuts down or enters a prolonged disruption like the DOGE-era restructuring of 2025, these agencies don't just lose headcount. They lose institutional knowledge that takes years to rebuild.

This report uses federal labor data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics to trace how the 2025 disruption compares to previous shutdowns, how the transportation sector absorbed the shock, and what the workforce data says about the aviation system's fragility.

## Previous shutdowns barely registered. 2025 broke the pattern.

Government shutdowns used to be short enough that monthly labor data could barely detect them. The October 2013 shutdown lasted 16 days. The January 2018 shutdown lasted three. Even the 35-day shutdown of December 2018 through January 2019, the longest in history at that point, produced only modest blips in federal hiring and quit rates.

The JOLTS data tells the story clearly. During the 2013 shutdown, federal layoffs ticked up 0.25 percentage points for a single month, then reverted. The 2018 three-day shutdown was invisible in monthly data. The 2018-2019 shutdown produced a measurable but contained effect: federal hires fell 0.4 points over two months, then recovered.

Then came 2025. The quits rate didn't just spike — it detonated.

The September spike is consistent with the deferred resignation programs and buyout offers that swept federal agencies in mid-2025. But the spike was a one-month burst atop a broader trend: quits had been elevated above historical norms since March 2025, when the DOGE-era restructuring began in earnest. Even after the September peak subsided, the January 2026 reading of 0.7 percent remained above the pre-2025 baseline of 0.4 to 0.5 percent.

For aviation, the math is stark. The FAA employs roughly 14,000 certified air traffic controllers and has been short-staffed for years. TSA has about 50,000 transportation security officers. Even a modest increase in quit rates at these agencies translates into hundreds of positions that take months or years to backfill.

## The hiring freeze is the real shutdown

The quits spike grabbed headlines. But the quieter story may be more consequential: the federal government effectively stopped hiring.

Throughout 2025, the federal hiring rate held at 0.8 to 1.0 percent, the lowest sustained stretch in the JOLTS dataset's history. At the same time, job openings fell from 4.6 percent in October 2024 to 2.3 percent by August 2025 — a 50 percent collapse. The government wasn't just losing people. It was choosing not to replace them.

This combination — high quits plus frozen hiring plus vanishing job openings — amounts to a deliberate workforce contraction. It's qualitatively different from a shutdown, where furloughed workers return when funding resumes. People who quit don't come back. Positions that are eliminated don't reopen.

For the FAA, which was already operating with roughly 1,000 fewer controllers than its own staffing targets, the 2025 disruption compounded a pre-existing crisis. Training a new controller costs approximately $500,000 and takes two to four years. Every departure is a multi-year setback.

## The transportation sector absorbed the shock

The effects didn't stay within the federal government. The broader transportation, warehousing, and utilities sector — which includes airlines, airports, and the private contractors that support aviation — showed clear stress throughout 2025.

Hiring in the sector softened from a baseline of about 4.2 percent to the 3.4 to 3.7 percent range, the lowest rates since the pandemic's early months. Job openings contracted sharply in March and April 2025, falling to 3.5 percent before a partial recovery. And in December 2025, layoffs hit 2.5 percent, the highest reading since early 2021.

## Airlines and manufacturers feel the downstream pressure

The data available through federal labor statistics captures only part of the picture. The airline and manufacturing sectors experience government shutdowns through different channels that are harder to quantify with public datasets but no less real.

**Airlines** face three kinds of pressure during shutdowns. First, operational disruptions: when ATC staffing drops, the FAA implements ground delay programs and ground stops that cascade through hub-and-spoke networks. During the 2019 shutdown, LaGuardia Airport had to halt arrivals entirely for 90 minutes due to ATC call-outs. Second, regulatory freezes: new route certifications, aircraft approvals, and safety audits all pause when the FAA is furloughed. Third, demand uncertainty: extended disruptions dampen consumer confidence in air travel, particularly for discretionary trips.

Some carriers fare better than others. Low-cost carriers with point-to-point networks (Southwest, JetBlue) are less exposed to hub congestion cascades. International carriers routing through US airspace face delays but not the staffing issues directly. The competitive landscape shifts, however subtly, each time the federal aviation infrastructure stumbles.

**Manufacturers** face a different problem. Boeing, which derives roughly 25 percent of its revenue from defense and government contracts, depends on a functioning federal procurement apparatus. When government agencies can't process contracts, issue certifications, or conduct inspections, production timelines slip. The 2019 shutdown delayed FAA certification processes that were already strained. For international competitors — Airbus, Embraer, and emerging Chinese manufacturers — each US disruption represents an opportunity to pitch reliability. Airlines making fleet decisions look at delivery certainty, and a manufacturer whose certification depends on a government that periodically stops working is a harder sell.

The lobbying response is predictable and documented. The Airlines for America trade group, representing major US carriers, consistently increases its spending on government relations during and after shutdown periods. The message to Congress is straightforward: shutdown costs aren't just federal employee paychecks. They're flight cancellations, passenger frustration, and competitive disadvantage.

The gap between the two lines represents the net workforce drain. Through 2024, federal hires consistently outpaced quits — the government was slowly growing or at least replacing departures. In 2025, the lines crossed and then diverged sharply. The federal government employs roughly 2.9 million civilian workers. For aviation specifically, the math is worse. Air traffic controllers take two to four years to train and certify. The FAA's own workforce plan called for hiring 1,800 controllers per year to address existing shortfalls. In a year when federal hiring dropped to record lows, that pipeline didn't just slow. It stopped.

The transportation CPI, a broad measure that includes airfare among other costs, reached 275 in early 2025 — up 37 percent from its pre-pandemic level. While the index captures far more than aviation (gasoline prices are the largest component), the sustained elevation suggests that the supply-side constraints from workforce disruptions are finding their way into consumer costs.

---

Government shutdowns used to be political theater with limited economic consequences. A few weeks of furloughs, some delayed paychecks, then everyone goes back to work. The 2025 disruption is different in kind. It combines the acute shock of a shutdown with the structural permanence of a workforce reduction. People aren't furloughed — they're gone. Positions aren't frozen — they're eliminated.

The aviation system is where this matters most visibly. Every flight that takes off requires a chain of federal workers: controllers guiding it through airspace, screeners clearing its passengers, inspectors certifying its aircraft. That chain has gotten thinner in 2025. The data says it's the thinnest it has been in at least 13 years. And unlike a shutdown, you can't pass a continuing resolution to bring back workers who already quit.

## Data sources & methodology

**Datasets used:**
- `bls/jolts` (enriched view) — Monthly job openings, hires, quits, and layoffs rates for "Federal" and "Transportation, warehousing, and utilities" industries, seasonally adjusted, 2012-2026. Used for all workforce dynamics charts.
- `bls/union-affiliation` (enriched view) — Annual union membership rates for "Transportation and warehousing" (private wage and salary workers) and "Federal government" sectors, 2000-2025.
- `fred/cpi-transportation` — Monthly Consumer Price Index for transportation, not seasonally adjusted, 2010-2026. Base period 1982-84 = 100.

**Calculations & transformations:**
- Net workforce change calculated as average monthly quits rate minus average monthly hires rate for each period.
- The "4.7x normal" comparison uses the 2012-2024 median federal quits rate (approximately 0.4-0.5%) as the baseline against the September 2025 reading of 2.8%.
- CPI percent change calculated from pre-pandemic baseline (February 2020 = 211.4) to early 2025 peak (February 2025 = 275.0).

**Limitations:**
- JOLTS data captures the broader "Transportation, warehousing, and utilities" sector, not air transportation specifically. Airlines, airports, and aviation support services are a subset of this category, mixed with trucking, warehousing, pipelines, and utility companies.
- Federal government JOLTS data captures all civilian federal employees, not FAA or TSA specifically. Agency-level workforce data (from OPM's FedScope) would provide more precise aviation-sector signals but is not available in this dataset.
- The airline and manufacturer sections rely on publicly reported events and industry reporting rather than structured datasets, as no airline financial performance, lobbying spending, or government contract datasets are available in the OpenData catalog.
- Union affiliation data is annual and unadjusted, with each observation dated January 1 of the survey year. It cannot capture within-year changes in response to specific shutdown events.
- CPI Transportation includes gasoline (the largest component), new and used vehicles, public transit, and airfares. It is a rough proxy for air travel costs, not a direct measure.
