Atorvastatin, a cholesterol pill that costs about $60 a year per patient, was prescribed to 16.8 million Americans through Medicare in 2023. It racked up 68.5 million prescription claims, more than any other drug in the program. Meanwhile, Eliquis, a blood thinner taken by fewer than 4 million people, cost the program $18.3 billion, almost as much as the next two most expensive drugs combined.

That gap tells the story of American prescription drugs in one sentence: doctors prescribe cheap generics by the hundreds of millions, but the money flows overwhelmingly to a small number of expensive brand-name medications. Medicare Part D, the federal prescription drug benefit covering roughly 50 million seniors and disabled Americans, spent $276 billion on drugs in 2023. Just ten medications accounted for more than a quarter of that total.

## Cholesterol and blood pressure drugs dominate the prescription pad

The most-prescribed medications in America read like a textbook for managing chronic disease in an aging population. The top ten drugs by prescription volume are all generics, and nearly all treat cardiovascular conditions: high cholesterol, high blood pressure, and diabetes.

Atorvastatin alone was prescribed to nearly 17 million people, one in three Medicare beneficiaries. Four of the top ten drugs treat high blood pressure. Eliquis (apixaban), a blood thinner made by Bristol-Myers Squibb and Pfizer, cost Medicare $18.3 billion in 2023, more than all ten of the most-prescribed generics combined. ## The $467 billion prescription market

Medicare Part D's $276 billion is a large piece of a much larger market. Total national spending on prescription drugs hit $467 billion in 2024, up from $122 billion in 2000. That's a 3.8-fold increase in 24 years. But even after adjusting for inflation, real drug spending roughly doubled, growing far faster than the general price level.

## Two parallel economies inside one formulary

Medicare Part D contains 3,598 unique drugs, but they operate in two fundamentally different economic regimes. A handful of metrics tells the whole story: generics account for more than 90 percent of prescriptions filled but only 12 percent of spending. Ultra-specialty drugs, those costing more than $50,000 per patient per year, serve fewer than one million people but consume more than a quarter of the entire $276 billion budget.

Combined, the five major GLP-1 drugs cost Medicare roughly $22 billion in 2023, serving about 3.3 million beneficiaries. To put that in perspective, Ozempic alone cost Medicare more than the program spent on atorvastatin, amlodipine, lisinopril, losartan, metoprolol, omeprazole, and metformin combined, drugs that together serve more than 70 million patients.

With Wegovy (the weight-loss version of semaglutide) gaining coverage and Medicare negotiating drug prices under the Inflation Reduction Act, this category will only grow.

The drugs doctors write most often, the statins and ACE inhibitors and metformins that keep tens of millions of older Americans healthy, barely register in the spending data. They're cheap, they're generic, and they work.

The spending is driven by a different set of drugs entirely: brand-name blood thinners, GLP-1 agonists, cancer drugs, and biologics that serve far fewer patients at far higher prices. Eliquis serves a quarter as many patients as atorvastatin but costs 18 times as much in total. Revlimid treats 37,000 people at $104,000 each.

Blood thinners prevent strokes. GLP-1 drugs help people manage diabetes. Cancer drugs extend lives. None of that is in dispute. What's in dispute is sustainability: whether a system that spends $467 billion annually on prescription drugs, where 50 medications drive more than half of Medicare's drug bill, can hold together as the population ages and new high-cost therapies reach the market. Atorvastatin will still cost $60 a year. The question is what happens to everything else. The IRA's negotiation authority is the first structural attempt at an answer.

## Data sources & methodology

**Datasets used:**
- `cms/part-d-spending` — Medicare Part D Spending by Drug, 2023 data. Includes total spending, claims, beneficiaries, cost per unit CAGR, and cost metrics for all drugs in the Part D formulary. Each row represents a unique drug with manufacturer-level and overall aggregates.
- `cms/nhe-by-category` — National Health Expenditure Accounts, 2000-2024. Used for total US prescription drug spending over time ($122B in 2000 to $467B in 2024).
- `fred/cpi` — Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Price Index (CPI-U), all items. Used to compute the inflation baseline: 2000 spending ($122B) compounded at actual CPI annual growth to show what spending would have been if it only tracked general inflation.
- `cms/ira-drug-prices` — CMS Maximum Fair Prices (negotiated under IRA), effective 2026. Contains the 10 drugs selected for the first round of Medicare drug price negotiations with their negotiated 30-day supply prices.

**Calculations & transformations:**
- Inflation baseline calculated as: $122B (2000 NHE Rx spending) × (CPI_year / CPI_2000). CPI_2000 = 172.2. This gives the counterfactual spending path if Rx had only grown at the general inflation rate.
- Tier categorization based on avg_spending_per_beneficiary: Generic (under $500), Brand ($500-$5K), Specialty ($5K-$50K), Ultra-specialty (over $50K). Share percentages computed from sum of total_beneficiaries and total_spending within each tier.
- IRA percent reductions from CMS August 2024 fact sheet comparing list prices to negotiated Maximum Fair Prices on a per-30-day-supply basis.
- CAGR scatter: `cagr_cost_per_unit` is the 5-year compound annual growth rate (2019-2023) in cost per dosage unit, computed by CMS. Total spending displayed in billions. Patient counts in thousands.
- GLP-1 total spending calculated by summing all semaglutide, tirzepatide, dulaglutide, and liraglutide formulations (Ozempic, Trulicity, Mounjaro, Rybelsus, Victoza 2-Pak and 3-Pak, Xultophy). Total: approximately $22 billion across 3.3 million beneficiaries.
- Opioid prescriptions aggregated across all formulations of hydrocodone, oxycodone, tramadol, fentanyl, morphine, codeine, and buprenorphine.
- Spending concentration calculated as sum of top N drugs' total_spending divided by total program spending ($275.9 billion). Top 10 = $71.1B (25.8%). "Next 40" spending estimated from the remaining top 50 minus top 10.
- Per-patient costs from `avg_spending_per_beneficiary` field. This represents Medicare program spending, not necessarily out-of-pocket patient costs.

**Limitations:**
- Medicare Part D covers approximately 50 million beneficiaries, predominantly seniors and disabled Americans. The prescribing picture for the commercially insured population (younger, working-age Americans) would differ, showing more antidepressants, birth control, stimulants, and fewer cardiovascular drugs.
- Total beneficiary counts across drugs sum to more than 50 million because individuals take multiple medications.
- Spending represents what Medicare paid (including plan and beneficiary contributions), not manufacturer revenue.
- NHE data tracks total national expenditure from all payers (Medicare, Medicaid, private insurance, out-of-pocket), not just Medicare Part D.
- IRA "before" prices are CMS's published list prices from their August 2024 fact sheet. Actual net prices after rebates were lower, so the real savings to Medicare may be less dramatic than the percent reductions suggest.
- The `cagr_cost_per_unit` field covers 2019-2023. Drugs with fewer than 5 years of Part D data have no CAGR value.
- GLP-1 weight-loss prescribing (Wegovy) is nearly invisible in this data ($200K total) because Medicare Part D did not broadly cover weight-loss indications in 2023. Coverage expanded in 2024.
- Drugs with fewer than 30 beneficiaries are excluded by CMS for privacy.
