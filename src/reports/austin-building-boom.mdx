Between 2010 and 2024, Austin added nearly 190,000 residents to a city that started the decade with just over 800,000. Construction cranes became as much a part of the skyline as the Capitol dome. The city issued more than half a million building permits in a single decade, tearing down older homes and replacing them with condos, duplexes, and apartment towers.

But growth at that pace leaves marks. Thousands of affordable housing units have already lost their income restrictions, with thousands more set to expire. Pedestrian deaths have climbed steadily even as total traffic crashes declined. And the construction boom itself peaked years ago, with new building permits dropping 43% from their 2019 high. Austin built a boomtown. The question now is what kind of city it left behind.

## Austin gained 190,000 residents in 14 years, but the rush is fading

The early 2010s were Austin's breakout moment. The city grew at roughly 3% per year from 2011 to 2014, adding 20,000 to 26,000 people annually. That's the kind of growth rate you'd normally associate with a boomtown in a developing country, not a mid-size American city.

Then something shifted. Growth rates decelerated steadily after 2014, and by 2017-2019 Austin was adding people at barely 1-2% per year. The pandemic shaved the city's population in 2020 (the only year it actually shrank), and the recovery has been sluggish. In 2024, Austin grew by just 0.4%, roughly 4,000 people. For a city that was once adding 25,000 a year, that's a dramatic cooldown.

The metro area is still growing, but the city proper has shifted from boom to plateau. That context matters for everything that follows: most of Austin's construction infrastructure was built to serve a much faster rate of growth than the city now needs.

## New construction peaked in 2019 and hasn't recovered

Austin's construction machine went into overdrive in the mid-2010s and peaked in 2019, when the city issued 28,000 new construction permits in a single year. That's separate from remodels and repairs, which add another 25,000 annually. The total permit volume hit 75,000 in 2019.

Since that peak, new construction has fallen by 43%, dropping to roughly 16,000 permits in both 2023 and 2024. That's back to early-2010s levels, before the boom really took off. Rising interest rates, higher material costs, and a softening rental market all played a role.

But here's what makes Austin's building story more nuanced than a simple boom-and-bust: even as the number of permits fell, the projects got bigger.

## Fewer permits, but bigger projects: the multifamily shift

In 2010, new construction permits produced about 12,500 housing units. By 2022, that number had jumped to 91,200 units, even though the permit count was lower than its 2019 peak. The math tells the story: Austin shifted from single-family construction to massive multifamily projects.

Single-family houses still dominate the permit count: 63,400 permits since 2020. But a category called "five or more family buildings" tells the real density story. Just 5,350 of those permits produced a staggering 295,000 housing units. That's an average of 55 units per permit, compared to roughly one unit per single-family permit.

The gap between the two lines on the chart is Austin's densification story in a single image. In 2010, each new construction permit produced about 1.1 housing units. By 2022, that ratio had climbed to 3.75. Austin didn't just build more. It built bigger.

The growth wasn't spread evenly. Districts 1 and 2, covering north-central Austin and the southeast growth corridor, absorbed the lion's share of new construction since 2020, producing roughly 87,000 and 90,000 housing units respectively. District 8, covering southwest Austin and the Hill Country edge, built just 13,000 units. District 6 in the far northwest built even fewer.

That concentration has consequences. Districts absorbing the most construction are also dealing with the most disruption: more traffic, more demolitions, more strain on infrastructure. Meanwhile, the districts building the least are effectively outsourcing their growth to the rest of the city.

## The boom tore down what came before

Construction doesn't just add to a city. It also subtracts. Between 2010 and 2018 (the years for which Austin publishes demolition data), the city issued 10,560 demolition permits. That number more than doubled from 770 in 2010 to a peak of 1,737 in 2015, tracking almost perfectly with the population growth surge.

Total demolitions, where an entire structure gets razed, made up roughly half the activity each year. These are the visible scars of the boom: a modest 1960s bungalow torn down and replaced by a pair of $700,000 townhomes.

But where those demolitions happened and where affordable housing got built are two very different maps. District 10 in northwest Austin saw the most demolitions of any council district (2,607), yet received only 279 affordable units. District 9, covering the urban core and South Congress corridor, lost 2,084 structures to demolition but at least gained 4,171 affordable units. The districts with the most teardowns were rarely the ones getting the most affordable replacements.

The construction permit data tells us what replaced those structures. Districts 1 and 2 led in new construction permits since 2020, with 28,000 and 24,000 permits respectively. ## Austin's affordable housing is disappearing faster than it's being built

For all the construction activity, Austin's affordable housing picture is bleak. The city tracks 2,412 affordable housing developments. Of those, 1,250 projects covering 8,366 units have already lost their affordability restrictions. That's more projects in the "expired" category than in any other status.

The pipeline isn't filling the gap fast enough. Another 5,479 affordable units across 179 projects are set to lose their restrictions before 2035. The biggest cliff comes in 2024-2027, when roughly 2,900 units are at risk. For a city that's been building at a furious pace, remarkably little of that construction has been aimed at the people who need affordable housing the most.

## Growth made Austin's streets deadlier

More people, more construction, more traffic. Austin's crash data shows that the city's roads became significantly more lethal over the past decade, even as total crash counts actually declined.

In 2015, the first year with reliable data across all categories, Austin recorded 101 traffic fatalities. By 2022 that number had risen to 117, a record. The trend is made more alarming by the fact that total reported crashes fell from around 17,000 annually in the mid-2010s to 13,000-14,000 by 2021-2023. Fewer crashes, but deadlier ones.

## The boom built a different kind of city

Austin's building boom peaked five years ago. What it produced is a city that looks fundamentally different from the one that existed in 2010: denser, taller, and more expensive. Here's the balance sheet.

The neighborhoods that were demolished to make way for development aren't coming back. The affordable housing units that are expiring their income restrictions each year represent families being pushed further from the city core. And the pedestrian death toll suggests that a city built around construction sites and widening roads made choices about who bears the cost of all that growth.

Austin is still a boomtown in the popular imagination. The data says the boom peaked five years ago, and what's left is the sorting out.

## Data sources & methodology

**Datasets used:**
- `census/austin-population` (enriched view) -- Austin city population estimates, 2010-2024. Year-over-year growth rates from the growth view.
- `austin/construction-permits` -- All issued construction permits, 2010-2025. Queried with SQL for annual counts by work class (New, Remodel, Repair, Addition) and housing unit sums.
- `austin/construction-permits-new` -- Detailed permits since 2016. Used for permit class breakdowns and council district analysis.
- `austin/demolitions` -- Residential demolition permits, 2010-2018. Grouped by year and demolition category.
- `austin/affordable-housing` -- City affordable housing inventory (2,412 developments). Queried for status distribution, income tier breakdowns, council district allocation, and affordability expiration timeline.
- `austin/crash-reports` -- Police crash reports with fatality and injury counts, 2013-2024. Queried for annual trends, pedestrian/cyclist deaths, and construction zone crashes. Note: 2013 and 2024 appear to be partial years based on lower crash counts.
- `austin/traffic-incidents` -- Real-time traffic incident reports, 2018-2025.

**Calculations & transformations:**
- Housing units per permit ratio calculated as total housing units from new construction divided by new construction permit count per year.
- Affordable housing income tier totals aggregated across all completed projects. MFI percentages are based on HUD definitions of area median family income.
- Construction zone crashes identified via the `road_constr_zone_fl` boolean flag in crash reports.
- 2013 crash data and 2024 crash data excluded from some charts due to apparent partial-year coverage.

**Limitations:**
- Demolition data only covers 2010-2018; no recent demolition trends available.
- Construction permit valuation fields returned null across both permit datasets, preventing dollar-value analysis of the building boom.
- Housing unit sums in the detailed permits dataset (construction-permits-new) contain outlier records in 2017 and 2024 that inflate totals implausibly. The larger permits dataset (construction-permits) was used instead for cleaner trends.
- Affordable housing data is a snapshot of current status. Units shown as "expired" may have been renewed or replaced by other programs not captured in this dataset.
- Crash data reflects reported incidents only; minor crashes may go unreported.
