America filed 5.7 million new business applications in 2025. That is 57 percent more than any year before the pandemic.

The surge arrived in the summer of 2020, peaked at more than 600,000 applications in a single month, and then did something nobody predicted: it held. Five years later, applications are still running at a pace that would have been unrecognizable in 2019. Politicians and pundits cite the number as proof of American economic dynamism. And the top-line figure is genuinely striking.

But the data underneath that headline complicates the picture considerably. The entire surge is non-corporate: sole proprietors, single-member LLCs, freelancers. Corporate filings, the kind that signal a business planning to hire employees and build something durable, have been flat at roughly 550,000 a year for two decades. Their share of total applications has fallen from one in three to fewer than one in ten.

What the US is experiencing is a mass migration into self-employment entity formation, not a small-business boom in the traditional sense. More EIN filings, more LLCs, more paperwork. Whether that translates into more businesses, more jobs, or more economic output is a different question, and one the data can actually answer.

## Five million applications a year, and it never went back down

For 15 years before the pandemic, business applications ran in a narrow band between 2.4 million and 3.5 million per year. The trend was gently upward, but nothing dramatic. Then 2020 happened.

April 2020 was the sharpest single-month drop in the history of the series, a 22 percent decline as lockdowns froze economic activity. But by July of that year, applications had already surpassed pre-pandemic levels. By March 2021, the country was filing more than 600,000 applications in a single month, roughly double the typical monthly rate from 2019.

The rebound wasn't temporary. Each year from 2020 through 2025 has exceeded 5 million total applications, and the pre-pandemic ceiling of 3.5 million now looks like a relic from a different economy. Something structural shifted, and the composition of these filings reveals what.

## Corporate filings flatlined. Everything else tripled.

The composition of that surge reveals the first complication. A corporate application signals intent to create a formal entity with shareholders, governance, and typically employees. A non-corporate filing can be anything from a consulting LLC to a DBA for an Etsy storefront or a weekend dog-walking service.

In 2006, corporate applications made up about one in three filings. By 2025, they account for fewer than one in ten. The corporate line is essentially flat across the entire 20-year window, hovering between 530,000 and 610,000 regardless of economic conditions. It barely flinched during the pandemic surge.

The non-corporate line, meanwhile, went from 1.75 million to 5.15 million. That is the entire story of the "entrepreneurship boom" in one chart. And this trend predates the pandemic. Non-corporate filings were already climbing steadily through the 2010s, driven by the rise of gig platforms, online commerce, and the growing popularity of the LLC as a legal structure. The pandemic poured accelerant on a fire that was already burning.

To be clear, the absolute number of high-propensity applications did grow modestly, from about 1.55 million in 2006 to 1.72 million in 2025, an 11 percent increase. But total applications nearly doubled over the same period, so the serious filers got diluted by a flood of non-employer entities. The applications with planned wages, the strongest signal of hiring intent, have been essentially flat at around 500,000 to 650,000 for two decades.

This is a critical distinction. When someone cites 5.7 million business applications as evidence of entrepreneurial dynamism, roughly 4 million of those applications show no characteristics that predict they will ever hire a single person.

The 2021 peak of 991,000 establishment births was genuinely impressive. It was the highest single year in the 30-plus year history of the BLS series, well above the previous record set during the dot-com era. But by 2024, births had dropped to 824,000, a 17 percent decline from the peak and only modestly above the pre-pandemic average of roughly 850,000.

The gap between the Census application numbers and the BLS birth numbers puts the boom in perspective. In 2024, the US filed 5.24 million business applications. Roughly 824,000 new establishments actually showed up in payroll data.

That is a conversion rate of about 16 percent. For every six people who filed paperwork to start a business, five never showed up in payroll records.

Net creation, the gap between births and deaths, has been consistently positive throughout the series. Even in the depths of the Great Recession, more businesses were born than died. But the gap is narrowing back toward its pre-pandemic average, suggesting that the burst of creative energy from 2020 to 2022 was more of a temporary acceleration than a permanent shift.

## Where entrepreneurs are filing vs. where they're building

Wyoming has 580,000 residents and posted 225 percent growth in business applications since 2019. Its residents did not suddenly become the most entrepreneurial people in America. The state has become one of the country's most popular destinations for LLC registration services, and its surge tells you more about legal structure shopping than about economic activity. Across the country, the states with the fastest growth are not necessarily the states producing the most meaningful businesses.

This is the finding that puts the entire business formation surge in its starkest context. The US filed 5.7 million EIN applications last year. But the share of its workforce that is actually self-employed barely moved. What is growing is entity formation: LLC registrations, EIN filings, the administrative paperwork of self-employment in an economy that increasingly routes independent work through formal business structures.

The post-pandemic surge was real, and it represents something genuinely new in the US economy. But 84 percent of those applications never show up in payroll data. The states with the fastest growth are often producing paper companies, not local employers. Actual firm births have already peaked and are declining. 5.7 million applications. The number is impressive. What it measures is paperwork.

## Data sources & methodology

**Datasets used:**

- `census/business-formation-weekly`: Weekly EIN applications by state, 2006-2026. Aggregated to annual totals for charts 1-3 and the state scatter plot. High-propensity defined by Census Bureau as applications with characteristics predictive of employer business formation.
- `bls/business-dynamics`: Annual establishment births and deaths from the Business Dynamics Statistics program, 1992-2024. Series IDs BDS0000000000000000111502LQ5 (births) and BDS0000000000000000111702LQ5 (deaths), quarterly values summed to annual. Values in thousands.
- `census/statistics-of-us-businesses`: Firm size, employment, and annual payroll by state and NAICS code, 2018-2022. Average pay computed as (annual_payroll \* 1000) / employment. National totals using naics code '00'.
- `worldbank/wdi`: Self-employment rate by country (indicator SL.EMP.SELF.ZS), ILO modeled estimates, 2000-2024. Sampled at 4-year intervals for chart readability.

**Calculations:**

- Growth rates: (2024 total - 2019 total) / 2019 total, computed from state-level annual aggregates
- High-propensity share: high_propensity_applications / total_business_applications per state per year
- Non-corporate applications: total_business_applications - corporate_applications
- Wage gap widening: computed from SUSB average pay per employee, comparing the difference between firms with fewer than 20 employees and firms with 5,000 or more employees in 2018 ($20,172) vs 2022 ($25,325)

**Limitations:**

- Business applications (Census BFS) measure intent to operate, not actual business formation. An EIN application is a piece of paper, not a firm.
- High-propensity classification is a Census Bureau model prediction, not an observed outcome. Its accuracy for post-pandemic cohorts has not been publicly validated.
- BLS Business Dynamics Statistics have an 18-24 month publication lag. The 2024 data is preliminary.
- Wyoming, Delaware, and Nevada business formation numbers are heavily influenced by out-of-state LLC and corporate registrations, which inflate their apparent entrepreneurship rates.
- SUSB excludes self-employed sole proprietors without paid employees, so the wage data only covers employer firms.
- Self-employment rates from the World Bank/ILO are modeled estimates that may not fully capture gig platform workers who file as independent contractors but do not self-identify as self-employed in household surveys.
