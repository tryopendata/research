The United States spent $15,474 per person on healthcare in 2024. Switzerland, the second-most-expensive system in the world, spent $10,602. That's a 46 percent gap. Japan spent $5,846, roughly a third of the US figure, and its citizens live six years longer.

American healthcare spending hit $5.3 trillion in 2024, consuming roughly 18 percent of GDP. That's more than the entire GDP of Germany. If US healthcare were its own economy, it would be the fourth largest in the world, behind only the US itself, China, and the EU. And somehow, despite this extraordinary expenditure, Americans die younger than citizens of every other wealthy democracy.

## The US is a massive outlier on spending

Among the 25 countries that spend more than $5,000 per person on healthcare, the United States is so far ahead that the chart needs a break in the axis to accommodate it. The gap between the US and the second-highest spender is larger than the gap between second place and 15th place.

That $13,473 per person is the 2023 OECD figure; the most recent CMS estimate puts 2024 US spending at $15,474. Either way, the trajectory is the same: US per-capita spending nearly doubled from $8,377 in 2010 to $15,474 in 2024. The pandemic accelerated the trend, but even before COVID, healthcare spending was growing faster than GDP, faster than wages, and faster than inflation.

## More money, worse outcomes

If spending bought outcomes, Americans would be the healthiest people on Earth. They're not. The US ranks near the bottom of wealthy nations on life expectancy, and the gap has been widening.

## The US has a delivery problem, not a quality problem

The paradox is that American medical technology and clinical quality are genuinely excellent. The US leads the world in cancer survival rates, medical research spending, and pharmaceutical innovation. If you have a complex or rare condition and have good insurance, America is one of the best places on Earth to receive treatment.

The problem is how the system delivers, finances, and administers that care. Multiple analyses have identified several drivers of the spending gap:

**Administration and insurance overhead** consume roughly 8 percent of US healthcare spending, compared to 1-3 percent in single-payer systems. The US has thousands of insurers, each with different billing codes, prior authorization requirements, and coverage rules. The administrative burden falls on providers too: American doctors spend significantly more time on paperwork than their counterparts in other countries.

**Pharmaceutical pricing** is dramatically higher. The same drugs cost 2-3 times more in the US than in peer nations, because the US is the only major economy where the government doesn't negotiate drug prices at a national level.

**Fee-for-service incentives** reward volume over outcomes. American doctors and hospitals are largely paid per procedure, creating incentives for more tests, more scans, more surgeries, whether or not they improve outcomes.

The trajectory is relentless. Even adjusting for inflation, healthcare spending has grown roughly 4-5 percent annually, consistently outpacing GDP growth and wage growth. The gap between the actual spending line and the inflation baseline is the excess: $3,300 per person per year that cannot be explained by general price increases. At this rate, healthcare will consume 20 percent of GDP by the early 2030s.

---

The United States doesn't have a healthcare quality problem. Its doctors, hospitals, and research institutions are world-class. What it has is a healthcare delivery problem: a system designed around private insurance, fee-for-service payment, and unregulated pharmaceutical pricing that generates extraordinary spending without extraordinary results.

## Data sources & methodology

**Datasets used:**
- `owid/healthcare-spending` — Healthcare spending per capita by country, annual. Source: OECD Health Statistics, WHO.
- `owid/life-expectancy` — Life expectancy at birth by country, annual. Source: UN World Population Prospects.
- `cms/health-expenditure` — US national healthcare expenditure, per capita and total spending, annual, 1960-2024. Source: Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services.

**Calculations & transformations:**
- Spending comparison uses 2023 OECD data (most recent complete year for international comparison). The opening paragraph references the 2024 CMS figure of $15,474, which uses a different accounting methodology than OECD figures; the first chart's $13,473 is the 2023 OECD figure.
- Life expectancy scatter uses 2022 data (most recent year with matched spending and life expectancy for most countries).
- US spending trend uses CMS National Health Expenditure data, which differs slightly from OECD figures due to accounting methodology.
- CPI-adjusted inflation baseline assumes 2.7% annual growth from the 2010 base of $8,377, consistent with the Federal Reserve's long-run PCE inflation average for the 2010-2024 period.
- Peer average for the synthesis chart is calculated from the 18 peer nations in the scatter plot (average $7,450 per capita, average life expectancy ~82.0 years).
- Administrative overhead percentages sourced from Himmelstein et al. (NEJM 2020) and OECD Health Statistics. Pharmaceutical spending per capita from OECD. Physician visit data from OECD Health at a Glance.

**Limitations:**
- Healthcare spending figures use current US dollars, not PPP-adjusted, which slightly overstates the spending of countries with higher price levels.
- Life expectancy is influenced by many factors beyond healthcare (diet, violence, accidents, inequality). The US gap is partly explained by higher obesity rates, drug overdose deaths, and gun violence.
- International spending comparisons are complicated by different healthcare system structures. The US includes private insurance overhead that other countries don't have.
