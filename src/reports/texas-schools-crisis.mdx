In November 2024, Austin voters approved a property tax increase for their schools. Fifty-eight percent said yes. They expected the money to fund teacher raises, fix aging buildings, and expand mental health services.

Instead, roughly 76 cents of every new dollar left the district entirely.

The money went to the state of Texas through a mechanism called "recapture." Austin ISD's total recapture obligation now exceeds $800 million a year. That's more than the district's entire instructional budget. And Austin is not alone. More than 240 school districts across Texas pay recapture, sending $6 billion annually to a system designed to equalize funding between rich and poor districts. Whether it's working as intended or has become something else depends on who you ask.

At the same time, nearly half a million Texas students have shifted into charter schools, many of which serve predominantly low-income families. The state's NAEP math scores have dropped 21 points since 2011, the steepest decline among large states. And the traditional public schools that still educate 5 million children are caught between two forces: money flowing to the state through recapture, and students flowing to alternatives through choice. This report follows the money, the students, and the test scores to understand what's happening to Texas public education.

## Charter enrollment has grown 40-fold in 25 years

When Texas authorized charter schools in 1995, the idea was modest: small, innovative alternatives that would push traditional schools to improve. The first charters opened in 1998 with about 12,000 students statewide. By 2023, that number was nearly 484,000, across more than a thousand campuses.

That 8.7 percent statewide figure understates the reality in cities. In Texas's large urban centers, charter schools now enroll roughly 1 in 6 students. In Travis County, home to Austin, the charter share hit 16.8 percent in 2023, up from 2.6 percent in 2005. Rural charter penetration remains below 4 percent. Charter schools are overwhelmingly an urban phenomenon, and the cities where they're growing fastest are the same ones losing the most traditional enrollment.

Texas's total K-12 enrollment has actually grown over this period, from 4.3 million to 5.5 million. The state's population is booming. So urban enrollment losses don't mean fewer students in Texas. They mean students are going somewhere else: suburbs, charters, private schools, neighboring districts. Not all of it is charter competition. Rising housing costs push families out of city centers. Demographic patterns shift.

But for the urban ISDs, the cause matters less than the effect. Fewer students means less state funding. Fixed costs for buildings, buses, and administration don't shrink proportionally. And then there's recapture.

## Where the money goes

Texas school funding runs on a mechanism called "recapture," sometimes called Robin Hood. Property-wealthy districts share their local tax revenue with poorer ones to equalize educational opportunity. Without it, the funding gap between Highland Park ISD and a rural West Texas district would be enormous. The principle is sound. The scale is the problem.

Austin ISD's total recapture obligation grew from $181 million in FY2015 to a peak of $901 million in FY2023, dropping to $665 million in FY2024 before climbing back to $821 million in the adopted FY2025 budget. The district sends more money to the state than it spends on classroom instruction. (A note on these numbers: they come from Austin ISD's own budget documents, not TEA's PEIMS accounting system. PEIMS records only the cash portion of recapture, which was $635 million in 2023. The larger figure includes "tax base assignments" where the state redirects a share of Austin's property tax base to other districts before Austin ever collects it. Both numbers are real. The budget figure better reflects the total cost to Austin taxpayers.)

Three policy moments explain the shape of this chart. In 2006, HB 1 compressed school district tax rates in response to a court ruling that the old system amounted to an unconstitutional state property tax. That kept recapture near zero for years. Then around 2011, Austin's property values started their historic run. As home prices surged, the gap between what Austin collected and what the state formula said it was entitled to widened fast, dragging recapture upward. By 2017 the rolling average hit 39 percent. In 2019, HB 3 was supposed to fix this: it restructured the recapture formula and pumped $6.5 billion into school funding statewide. You can see a brief dip. But Austin's property values kept climbing faster than the relief could offset, and by 2022 recapture was back above pre-reform levels. It now sits above 50 percent.

When Austin voters passed Proposition A in November 2024, raising the M&O tax rate by 9.1 cents, they were told the $171 million in new revenue would fund teacher raises and expanded services. What many didn't realize: roughly $130 million of that, 76 percent, would go straight to recapture. Austin keeps about $41 million.

Where does the recaptured money actually go? Legally, it flows into the Foundation School Fund, the state's main pot for distributing money to school districts. Some genuinely reaches poorer districts. But critics argue the state uses recapture to reduce its own contribution: each local dollar that enters the fund allows the legislature to redirect a general revenue dollar elsewhere. The Texas School Coalition calls it a shell game. TEA counters that the fund is a single pool and the money reaches students regardless of its source.

The burden is also concentrated. Using TEA's standardized PEIMS accounting (which captures fund transfers but not tax base assignments), Dallas ISD led the state with $232 million in recapture in 2023, followed by Austin ISD at $175 million. Highland Park, Plano, and Cypress-Fairbanks each sent over $140 million. These aren't all wealthy enclaves. Dallas ISD serves a majority-minority, majority-low-income population, yet its rapidly rising property values make it a net exporter of education dollars.

The number keeps growing because property values rise while the state's basic allotment per student has been largely flat, barely adjusted for inflation since 2019. Charter schools, notably, are exempt from recapture entirely. They receive state formula funding but generate almost no local property tax revenue, so there's nothing to recapture. In 2024, charters received $4.7 billion in state funding while contributing just $317 million in local revenue. Traditional districts generated $44.9 billion locally but had to send $6 billion of it back.

And charter's slice of the pie keeps growing. In 2004, charter schools received about 1.4 percent of all state education dollars. By 2023, that share had risen to 8.5 percent. That's not because the total shrank. State funding grew for both systems. But charter funding grew six times faster, driven by enrollment that tripled from 153,000 students in 2004 to nearly 484,000 in 2023. Every student who moves from a traditional ISD to a charter takes their per-pupil state funding with them, but the ISD's fixed costs (buildings, bus routes, pension obligations) don't shrink proportionally.

### A tale of two districts, ten miles apart

Recapture is supposed to level the playing field. Eanes ISD and Austin ISD test that theory. The two districts share a metro area. Their boundary lines are roughly ten miles apart. But they occupy different financial universes.

Eanes ISD covers Westlake Hills and Rollingwood, some of the most expensive real estate in Texas. It enrolls about 7,700 students. Austin ISD serves the city proper, about 73,000 students, increasingly low-income. Both are Chapter 49 recapture districts. Both send hundreds of millions to the state every year. That's where the similarity ends.

## What the data does and doesn't prove

These trends, rising recapture, growing charter enrollment, declining test scores, an increasingly low-income student body, are all happening at once. The data shows correlation, not causation. COVID learning loss, teacher shortages, demographic change, and curriculum shifts all contributed to the test score decline independently of funding dynamics. Anyone who claims a single policy caused the decline is oversimplifying.

But the structural tension is real. Property-wealthy urban districts generate substantial local tax revenue. The state takes a growing share through recapture. The legislature hasn't meaningfully increased its per-student basic allotment since 2019. Charters receive state dollars but are exempt from recapture. And each student who leaves a traditional district takes per-pupil funding while the district's fixed costs remain.

The policy question isn't whether charters work for the students who attend them. KIPP Houston and IDEA in the Rio Grande Valley have shown they can. The question is whether the funding mechanism creates a structural disadvantage for the traditional schools that still serve 91 percent of Texas students.

---

There is no single villain. Charter schools serve real families making rational choices. The best charter networks have produced genuine gains for disadvantaged students. Recapture was designed to address real inequities. The legislature faces real budget constraints.

But the math doesn't care about intentions. Recapture bills rise with property values while per-student allotments stagnate. Charter enrollment grows by 40,000 students a year while traditional districts absorb the same loss. The state counts recaptured local dollars as its own contribution, quietly shifting the education burden to property taxpayers.

Austin voters approved a tax increase for their schools and watched three-quarters of it leave. Test scores that once led the nation now trail it. And 484,000 students have moved to alternatives while the system serving the other 5 million grows more strained.

The data can't prove that any single policy caused the decline. But it can show that all the pressures point in the same direction, and that the students with the fewest options are absorbing the most cost. Whether that's acceptable is not a data question. It's the most expensive political question in Texas.

## Data sources & methodology

**Datasets used:**
- `nces/ccd` (Common Core of Data) -- School-level enrollment, charter status, free/reduced-price lunch eligibility, urban locale codes, and district identifiers for all Texas K-12 schools. Years covered: 1986-2023. Texas identified by FIPS code 48. Charter status determined by the `charter` field (1 = charter, 0 = traditional).
- `tea/public-school-finance` (PEIMS) -- 136M rows of Texas district-level financial data. Recapture payments identified via object code 3480. Revenue sources identified by object code ranges (5700s = local, 5800s = state, 5900s = federal). Years covered: 2011-2024.
- `tea/charter-school-finance` (PEIMS) -- 4.5M rows of charter school financial data following the same PEIMS coding structure. Years covered: 1998-2024.
- `nces/naep` -- National Assessment of Educational Progress scores for grades 4 and 8 in math and reading. Texas vs. national public school averages, 2003-2024.
- `nces/sat-scores` -- State-level SAT total, ERW, and math scores plus participation rates. 2017-2023 (post-redesign only).
- `nces/act-scores` -- State-level ACT composite scores and participation rates. 2012-2023.
- `nces/teacher-salary-by-state` -- Average teacher salary by state, 1969-2022, in both current and inflation-adjusted (2021-22 constant) dollars.
- `tea/accountability-ratings` -- TEA accountability ratings (A-F) for all Texas school districts and campuses, 2023-24. Includes charter flag, economic disadvantage rates, and component scores.
- `nces/district-enrollment` -- Public school enrollment by grade, race/ethnicity, and sex for 2024-25. Used for district-level demographic snapshots.

- `tea/recapture` -- Clean recapture payment time series by district, 1994-2026, sourced from TEA Excess Local Revenue reports.

Austin ISD recapture figures in this report use the district's *total recapture obligation*, sourced from:

- [Austin ISD Budget Reports](https://www.austinisd.org/budget/reports) — official budget books with revenue/expenditure detail
- [TEA Excess Local Revenue reports](https://tea.texas.gov/finance-and-grants/state-funding/excess-local-revenue) — district-level recapture data ([2023-24 PDF](https://tea.texas.gov/finance-and-grants/state-funding/excess-local-revenue/2023-2024-excess-local-revenue-district.pdf))
- KUT Austin reporting: [recapture nears $1 billion (June 2023)](https://www.kut.org/education/2023-06-23/austin-isd-school-board-approves-deficit-budget-as-recapture-payment-nears-1-billion), [Prop A tax election (Oct 2024)](https://www.kut.org/education/2024-10-25/voters-tax-rate-increase-austin-schools-group-of-former-trustees-recapture-aisd)
- [Austin Monitor: former trustees raise concerns (Oct 2024)](https://austinmonitor.com/stories/2024/10/as-voters-weigh-higher-tax-rate-for-austin-public-schools-group-of-former-trustees-raises-concerns/)

This figure includes both cash payments and "tax base assignments" (where the state redirects a portion of Austin's property tax base to other districts before collection). TEA's PEIMS accounting system records only the cash portion of recapture. For FY2022-23, the difference is significant: Austin ISD's total obligation was $901 million, while PEIMS object code 7915 (fund 699) shows $635 million in cash payments and object code 3480 shows $174 million in fund transfers. We use the budget document figure because it better reflects the total cost to Austin taxpayers, but readers should understand these are district-reported numbers, not independently audited TEA figures.

The recapture-as-percentage-of-property-tax chart uses the same budget document methodology: total recapture obligation divided by total local property tax revenue. PEIMS-derived percentages would be lower (roughly 18-22% in 2023 vs. the 36% shown) because the numerator excludes tax base assignments.

The Sankey diagram's upstream flows (property taxes → recapture split) are based on [Austin ISD's published Prop A revenue estimates](https://www.austinisd.org/propa) ($171M total, $130M to recapture). The downstream split (Foundation School Fund → "Poorer Districts" vs. "Displaces State Spending") is an editorial estimate based on analysis by the [Texas School Coalition](https://www.txsc.org/schoolfinance/) and [RecaptureTexas.org](https://recapturetexas.org/). These are advocacy organizations that oppose the current recapture structure. TEA and legislative supporters argue that recapture money goes into the Foundation School Fund and reaches students in lower-wealth districts as intended. The actual fiscal displacement effect is debated and likely varies by legislative session and budget year. The Sankey represents the critics' argument, not a TEA accounting statement.

**Calculations & transformations:**
- Charter enrollment share calculated as charter enrollment / total enrollment per year per locale category
- Urban locale codes grouped: 11-13 = City, 21-23 = Suburb, 31-33 = Town, 41-43 = Rural (NCES Urban-Centric Locale Classification)
- Free/reduced-price lunch percentages calculated at the state level per year. The jump around 2012 reflects expanded Community Eligibility Provision (CEP) enrollment under the Healthy, Hunger-Free Kids Act
- District-level enrollment includes all schools within the LEA boundary, including district-operated charters but not independent charter operators in the same geography. The Travis County charter enrollment figure captures all charter operators within the county, providing a more complete picture of charter competition for Austin ISD.

**Limitations:**
- STAAR test score data (Texas's state assessment) is not available in the NCES datasets and could not be broken down by charter vs. traditional school type. NAEP data provides state-level comparisons but not within-state district-type breakdowns.
- SAT state-level data does not include demographic breakdowns; race/ethnicity splits are national only.
- Austin ISD recapture figures use district-reported total obligation (cash + tax base assignments), not TEA PEIMS cash-only figures. The two measures diverge significantly: $901M vs. $635M for 2023. The statewide recapture chart now uses the `tea/recapture` dataset (TEA's official recapture-by-district report, 1994-2026), which provides clean total recapture per district. These figures are higher than the PEIMS object code 3480 approach used in earlier versions of this report because they include tax base assignments alongside cash payments. The proportion of recapture that "displaces" state spending vs. genuinely redistributes to poorer districts is an analytical judgment, not a precise accounting.
- CCD enrollment data counts students at a point in time (fall snapshot) and may not capture mid-year transfers. The 2020 enrollment dip reflects pandemic disruptions, not necessarily permanent departures.
- Teacher salary data from NCES uses CPI-adjusted 2021-22 constant dollars. The dataset provides snapshots at roughly decade intervals (1969-70, 1979-80, etc.), not annual time series. The salary figures are averages across all public school teachers in a state, not weighted by district size or experience level.
- TEA accountability ratings are district-level (campus_number IS NULL). Charter vs. ISD percentages computed from 178 charter districts and 1,020 traditional ISD districts. The B/C/D percentages in the chart were computed from raw counts. The "economically disadvantaged" average is unweighted across districts (small and large districts count equally).
- Demographic data from `nces/district-enrollment` covers 2024-25 only; historical racial composition trends are not available from this dataset.
- The `nces/district-staff` dataset covers 2024-25 only; no historical staffing trend is available.
- Dropout racial disparity: dropout counts from `nces/district-dropout` (2023-24, grades 9-12, Texas, Category Set A by race/ethnicity). Enrollment denominator from `nces/district-enrollment` (2024-25, grades 9-12, Male + Female). These are different school years, so the disparity ratios are approximate. NCES suppresses small cell sizes for privacy, so some minority group counts may be undercounted.
