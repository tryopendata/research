In 2003, Mississippi's 4th graders scored dead last in the nation on the NAEP math exam, 11 points below the national average. By 2024, they'd pulled nearly even, gaining 16 points while the rest of the country lost ground. Over the same period, Texas 8th graders went from 1 point above the national average to 3 points below, dropping 21 points from their 2011 peak. Florida's 8th grade reading scores fell 10.5 points in just five years.

The Nation's Report Card, the only standardized test given uniformly across all 50 states, tells a story that upends the conventional wisdom about American education. The states once held up as reform models are stumbling. The states written off as hopeless are climbing. And the COVID learning loss that dominated headlines turns out to be unevenly distributed in ways that reveal something deeper about which education strategies actually produce lasting gains.

## Mississippi pulled off something nobody expected

Mississippi was synonymous with last place in education for decades, and not without reason. In 2003, its 4th graders scored 223 in math, the lowest in the nation. Its 4th grade reading score of 206 was similarly bottom-of-the-barrel.

Then something changed. In 2013, Mississippi enacted the Literacy-Based Promotion Act, which required 3rd graders to demonstrate reading proficiency before advancing to 4th grade. The state invested in intensive reading instruction, teacher training, and early identification of struggling readers. Critics called it harsh. The results have been undeniable.

Mississippi climbed 13 points in 4th grade reading between 2003 and 2024. The national average fell 2 points over the same period. That's a 15-point relative swing, driven largely by gains after the literacy law took effect in 2015. The results at the elementary level were real: 4th grade math scores rose from 234 in 2003 to 246 in 2017, consistently outpacing the national average.

But that progress masked a growing problem in the upper grades.

Massachusetts pays teachers $89,000 in inflation-adjusted dollars and has the highest 8th grade math scores in the country. But South Dakota pays $50,000 and posts scores nearly as high. New York pays the most in the nation, $92,000, but sits 12 points below Massachusetts in math.

Florida's position is worth lingering on. It pays teachers $51,000 in real dollars, among the lowest in the country, and *less than it paid them 50 years ago* after adjusting for inflation. A Florida teacher in 1970 earned the equivalent of $63,000 in today's money. That decades-long erosion of real pay has consequences that compound: fewer talented people entering the profession, higher turnover, and an experience gap that shows up most clearly in the upper grades where subject expertise matters more.

Mississippi, the lowest-paying state at $47,000, is an obvious counterexample to the "just pay more" argument. It has dramatically improved outcomes while remaining at the bottom of the salary table. But that doesn't mean pay doesn't matter. It means that *how* you teach can overcome *how much* you pay for a while, especially at the elementary level. Whether Mississippi can sustain its gains as it asks teachers to do demanding, high-skill work for the lowest pay in the country is an open question.

---

The NAEP data tells a story that refuses to conform to easy political narratives. School choice hasn't prevented Florida's decline. High spending hasn't protected Massachusetts from COVID losses. Low spending hasn't stopped Mississippi from climbing.

What the data does suggest is that targeted, evidence-based interventions at the elementary level, particularly in literacy, produce real gains. Mississippi's reading reforms worked. But those gains face a harder test as students advance to middle school, where the curriculum demands shift and the strategies that moved the needle in early grades may not transfer.

The most sobering finding is also the simplest one. In 2003, the national 4th grade reading average was 217. In 2024, it's 214. After two decades, trillions of dollars in education spending, and countless reform initiatives, America's 9-year-olds read slightly worse than their counterparts did a generation ago. That's not a state-level problem. It's a national one.

## Data sources & methodology

**Datasets used:**
- `nces/naep` — NAEP (National Assessment of Educational Progress) scores by state, subject, and grade level, 2003-2024. The NAEP is administered biennially to nationally representative samples of students in participating schools. It was not administered in 2020 due to COVID-19.
- `nces/teacher-salary-by-state` — Average teacher salary by state in current and constant (inflation-adjusted) dollars, from NCES Digest of Education Statistics Table 211.60. Covers selected years from 1969-70 through 2021-22.

**Calculations & transformations:**
- Score changes are simple differences (2024 score minus 2003 or 2019 score). No adjustments for demographic shifts or enrollment changes.
- Teacher salaries use the "constant dollar" metric from NCES, which adjusts for inflation using the Consumer Price Index. The 2021-22 values are in 2022 dollars.
- State rankings use the full set of 51 jurisdictions (50 states plus DC).

**Limitations:**
- NAEP scores are on a 0-500 scale within each subject and grade, but are not directly comparable across subjects or grades. A 5-point gain in math does not necessarily represent the same magnitude of improvement as a 5-point gain in reading.
- NAEP was not administered in 2020, creating a gap during the critical early COVID period. The 2022 results capture the cumulative pandemic effect but miss the trajectory.
- Teacher salary data uses state averages, which mask significant within-state variation (urban vs. rural, district-level pay scales).
- Correlation between teacher pay and student outcomes is confounded by cost of living, state wealth, demographics, and numerous other factors. The scatter plot is descriptive, not causal.
- Mississippi's gains coincide with the Literacy-Based Promotion Act but also with other policy changes and demographic shifts. Causal attribution to any single policy should be made cautiously.
