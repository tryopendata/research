South Korea's fertility rate hit 0.72 children per woman in 2023. Not 1.72. Not 2.72. Zero point seven two. That's roughly one child for every three women over a lifetime, a number so far below the 2.1 replacement threshold that demographers initially questioned the data. It wasn't an error.

South Korea may be the most extreme case, but it's not an outlier. In 1960, just six countries on Earth had fertility rates below replacement level. Today, 129 of 236 do. More than half the world's nations are no longer producing enough children to maintain their populations. The global average fertility rate has fallen from 5.4 children per woman to 2.35 in six decades, a decline that took centuries in Europe but just a generation in much of Asia. This is the defining demographic event of the 21st century, and its economic consequences are only beginning to arrive.

## The steepest decline in human history

The speed of the drop is what makes this different from anything demographers have seen before. It took France 150 years to go from a fertility rate of 6 to 2. South Korea did it in 25 years. China did it in 15.

India crossed below replacement in 2020. That sentence alone would have been unthinkable to mid-20th century demographers who warned of an "Indian population bomb." The world's most populous country now has a fertility rate of 1.98, and it's still falling. Among the seven countries charted above, only Nigeria remains well above replacement, though even its rate has dropped from a peak of 6.85 in 1980 to 4.48.

The United States, often seen as a demographic exception among rich nations, has been below replacement since 2010. Its rate of 1.62 puts it below France, above Japan, and trending in the wrong direction.

## More than half the world has crossed the line

The raw count tells the story as clearly as any chart. In 1960, six countries were below replacement. By 2000, the number had reached 90. Today it stands at 129, more than half of all nations.

The global average fertility rate has fallen to 2.35 children per woman, barely above the replacement threshold of 2.1. That average is propped up almost entirely by sub-Saharan Africa. Remove the African continent from the calculation and the global figure drops well below replacement. The transition that took Europe a century is now running on fast-forward across Asia, Latin America, and the Middle East.

## Aging populations are already straining pension systems

Falling fertility doesn't just shrink populations. It reshapes them. When birth rates collapse, the age pyramid inverts: the base narrows as fewer children are born, while the top widens as existing adults grow old. Japan is the laboratory for this experiment, and the results are not encouraging.

In 1970, just 7 percent of Japan's population was over 65. Today that figure is nearly 30 percent. South Korea, which had an even younger population than Nigeria in 1970, now has 18.3 percent of its people over 65 and is aging faster than any country in history. By 2050, South Korea's elderly share is projected to surpass Japan's.

The economic math here is unforgiving. Every retiree needs to be supported by working-age taxpayers funding pensions, healthcare, and social services. When 1 in 3 people is retired, the burden on the remaining workers becomes enormous. Japan already spends more than a quarter of its GDP on social security. South Korea is watching its pension reserves drain toward insolvency by the 2050s.

China's trajectory may be the most consequential. Its elderly population has tripled as a share of total population since 1990, from 5 percent to 14 percent, and it's accelerating. China will grow old before it grows rich, a situation without precedent among major economies. By contrast, Nigeria's age structure has barely changed in 50 years.

## Can immigration fill the gap?

Some countries have tried to offset shrinking birth rates with immigration. The United States has been the most successful at this, absorbing 1.2 to 1.7 million net migrants per year through much of the 2000s and 2010s. Germany took in over a million people in 2015 alone during the Syrian refugee crisis. Even Japan, historically resistant to immigration, has quietly increased its intake.

But immigration is not a fix. It's a Band-Aid. Even the United States, which takes in more immigrants than the next several countries combined, has seen its fertility rate fall to 1.62. Immigrants' own birth rates tend to converge toward the host country's norms within a generation. And politically, mass immigration at the scale needed to offset fertility collapse is increasingly contentious in every wealthy democracy.

Japan's case is instructive: even after loosening immigration policy, the country added just 141,000 net migrants in 2025. With a population declining by roughly 500,000 per year, immigration doesn't come close to closing the gap.

## The world will peak, then shrink

The UN's medium-variant projection shows global population reaching roughly 10.3 billion around the mid-2080s, then beginning a decline. This would mark the first time in recorded history that the human population has shrunk from natural causes rather than war or plague.

But here's the thing about population projections: they tend to be too optimistic about fertility. The UN's 2019 revision projected a global population of 10.9 billion by 2100. The 2024 revision dropped that to 10.2 billion. Several independent models, including those from the Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation at the University of Washington, project the peak arriving sooner and lower, some as early as the 2060s at under 10 billion.

The speed of the decline after the peak depends almost entirely on what happens in sub-Saharan Africa. If African fertility follows the same trajectory as Asia's (and early signs suggest it will), the global population could be shrinking well before 2100.

---

The fertility collapse is not a crisis of one country or one continent. It is a species-level trend that touches every economy, every pension system, every labor market, and every family. There is no country that has reversed a sustained fertility decline through government policy. Not Singapore's baby bonuses. Not Hungary's tax breaks. Not Japan's childcare subsidies. Couples everywhere are making the same calculation: children are expensive, careers are demanding, and the old reasons to have large families no longer apply.

At 0.72 children per woman, South Korea would need to nearly triple its fertility rate just to reach replacement. At current rates, its population of 52 million would fall to roughly 25 million by 2100. That's not a demographic transition. It's a demographic vanishing act.

## Data sources & methodology

**Datasets used:**
- `owid/fertility` — Total fertility rate (children per woman) for 236 countries, 1960-2023. Based on UN World Population Prospects estimates.
- `un/population-prospects` — World population estimates (1950-2020) and medium-variant projections (2025-2100) from the UN Population Division.
- `owid/population-age` — Population by age group (under 5, 5-14, 15-24, 25-64, 65+) for all countries, used to compute age structure shares.
- `worldbank/net-migration` — Net migration by country, 2000-2025 estimates from the World Bank.

**Calculations & transformations:**
- Percent over 65 and under 15 computed as share of total population from age group data.
- Countries "below replacement" defined as total fertility rate under 2.1.
- Global average fertility computed as unweighted mean across 236 countries with ISO 3-letter codes (excludes regional aggregates).
- Net migration values rounded to nearest thousand for chart readability.

**Limitations:**
- UN fertility estimates for recent years (2022-2023) may be provisional and subject to revision.
- Population projections use the UN medium-variant scenario; actual outcomes depend on fertility, mortality, and migration assumptions.
- Global average fertility is an unweighted country mean, not population-weighted, which overstates the influence of small nations.
- Net migration data for 2025 is a UN/World Bank projection, not observed.

*Data accessed on 2026-03-24 via the [OpenData API](https://tryopendata.ai).*
